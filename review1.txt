\\================================================================================
BACKGROUND CONCEPTS: NEURAL NETWORKS, PDEs, AND DMD
================================================================================

1. WHAT DOES A NEURAL NETWORK DO?
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
At its core, a neural network is a "universal function approximator." You feed 
it an input (x), it passes that input through layers of interconnected nodes 
(neurons) multiplied by adjustable weights and biases, and it spits out a 
prediction (y). 

During training, the network calculates how wrong its prediction is using a 
"loss function" and uses optimization algorithms (like gradient descent) to 
tweak its weights. Over time, it learns the underlying mathematical mapping 
between inputs and outputs without being explicitly programmed with the rules.


2. WHAT IS A PHYSICS-INFORMED NEURAL NETWORK (PINN)?
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
A standard neural network requires massive amounts of data to learn a pattern. 
A PINN fixes this by embedding the laws of physics directly into the network’s 
loss function, usually in the form of differential equations.

The Damped Oscillator Example:
Imagine a mass on a spring bouncing in water. The physics governing its 
displacement x(t) over time is known: 

    m*(d^2x/dt^2) + c*(dx/dt) + k*x = 0

    Where:
    - m = mass
    - c = damping (water resistance)
    - k = spring stiffness

Instead of needing thousands of data points of the spring's position to learn 
its motion, a PINN is trained on just a few points (like its starting position). 
For any random time 't' it guesses, we calculate the derivatives (velocity and 
acceleration) using the network itself and plug them into the equation above. 
If the equation doesn't equal zero, the network penalizes itself. The physics 
acts as a strict guide, ensuring physically impossible predictions aren't made.


3. WHAT IS A PARTIAL DIFFERENTIAL EQUATION (PDE) & WHY DO WE SOLVE THEM?
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
A Differential Equation describes how a quantity changes relative to another 
variable (e.g., how position changes over time). A *Partial* Differential 
Equation (PDE) involves functions that change with respect to multiple 
independent variables—typically space (x, y, z) and time (t).

Why we solve them:
Almost all continuous physical phenomena are written in the language of PDEs:
- Fluid Dynamics: Weather forecasting and aerodynamics (Navier-Stokes).
- Heat Transfer: How heat spreads across a CPU or turbine blade (Heat Equation).
- Finance: How option prices change over time (Black-Scholes).

Solving a PDE means finding the exact mathematical function that satisfies 
these rate-of-change laws across a specific space and time.


4. WHAT ARE NUMERICAL PDE SOLVERS?
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Most real-world PDEs are too mathematically complex to solve exactly using pen 
and paper. Instead, we use "numerical solvers" to approximate the solution on 
a computer.

Finite Difference Method (FDM):
FDM is the simplest numerical approach. It breaks continuous space and time 
into a discrete grid of points (like pixels). Instead of looking at continuous 
curves, it approximates derivatives using the differences between neighboring 
grid points. 

For example, the velocity (first derivative) at grid point 'i' is approximated:
    du/dx ≈ (u_{i+1} - u_i) / delta_x

By setting up these simple algebraic relationships for every point on the grid, 
the computer solves a giant matrix of equations step-by-step to simulate the 
entire system.


5. WHAT IS DYNAMIC MODE DECOMPOSITION (DMD)?
--------------------------------------------------------------------------------
Imagine you have a complex, chaotic video of a fluid swirling behind a cylinder. 
The raw data contains thousands of moving pixels across hundreds of frames, 
making it incredibly hard to analyze mathematically.

DMD is a data-driven technique that takes these snapshots of data and extracts 
the dominant underlying patterns, called "modes." 

- Each mode represents a specific spatial structure (e.g., a repeating vortex).
- Each mode is tied to a specific "eigenvalue," which tells you exactly how 
  fast that pattern oscillates and whether it is growing, decaying, or staying 
  stable over time.

In short, DMD decomposes complex, chaotic data into a simple sum of individual 
waves, letting you see the underlying rhythm of a chaotic system.
